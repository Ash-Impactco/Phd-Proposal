Despite growing interest, critical uncertainties remain regarding CO‚ÇÇ removal rates, durability, and measurement protocols under real-world agricultural conditions. This PhD research will conduct the **first comprehensive field assessment of ERW on volcanic islands**, using S√£o Miguel Island (Azores, Portugal) as a natural laboratory.

3. What is the net CO‚ÇÇ removal when upstream emissions (grinding, transport) are included?
4. Can alkalinity export be measured with sufficient precision (<30% uncertainty) for carbon credit verification?

Expected outcomes include: (1) first quantitative field-based CO‚ÇÇ removal rates for volcanic island ERW, (2) validated MRV protocol for carbon credit certification, (3) 3-4 peer-reviewed publications in high-impact journals (*Environ. ## **1. BACKGROUND & SIGNIFICANCE**

### **1.1 The Climate Crisis and Carbon Dioxide Removal**

Limiting global warming to 1.5-2¬∞C requires both deep emissions reductions and large-scale carbon dioxide removal (CDR) (IPCC AR6, 2021). The IPCC projects that 100-1,000 GtCO‚ÇÇ of atmospheric removal will be necessary by 2100 to achieve net-zero emissions. Current CDR approaches (afforestation, direct air capture, bioenergy with CCS) face significant land-use, energy, or cost constraints.

**Enhanced Rock Weathering (ERW)** offers a promising alternative: it accelerates natural silicate mineral weathering‚Äîa geological CO‚ÇÇ sink that has regulated Earth's climate for billions of years‚Äîto anthropogenic timescales. By applying finely ground silicate rocks (basalt, olivine, wollastonite) to agricultural soils, ERW enhances mineral dissolution, generating alkalinity (HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª, CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª) that sequesters atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ during weathering reactions and exports durably to the ocean (Beerling et al., 2020).

### **1.2 Enhanced Rock Weathering: Theory and Uncertainty**

**Fundamental Weathering Reaction:**
```
Basalt (Ca,Mg silicates) + CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí Ca¬≤‚Å∫ + Mg¬≤‚Å∫ + HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª + SiO‚ÇÇ(aq) + clays
```

**Stoichiometry:** For each mole of divalent cation released, 1 mole of CO‚ÇÇ is consumed from the atmosphere and exported as bicarbonate alkalinity to the ocean.

**Key Uncertainties:**
1. **Weathering rates:** Laboratory studies show rapid dissolution (Gislason & Oelkers, 2003), but field rates are 10-100x slower due to surface passivation, low mineral:water ratios, and kinetic limitations (White & Brantley, 2003).

2. **Durability:** Does alkalinity export to the ocean (permanent sequestration), or does it precipitate as secondary carbonates (CaCO‚ÇÉ, MgCO‚ÇÉ) within the soil profile (Renforth et al., 2015)?

3. **Life cycle emissions:** Rock grinding consumes energy (40-80 kg CO‚ÇÇ/t basalt), potentially offsetting CO‚ÇÇ removal benefits at low weathering efficiencies (Strefler et al., 2018).

4. **MRV protocols:** Current carbon credit frameworks (Puro.earth, Verra) lack standardized measurement protocols for ERW, creating barriers to commercialization.

**Recent Field Studies:**
- Kelland et al. (2020): UK sorghum trial, 1.5 t basalt/ha ‚Üí 0.3-0.7 tCO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr removal (inferred from crop yield and soil chemistry)
- Haque et al. (2020): Ontario wollastonite trial, 8-20 t/ha ‚Üí 1.0-4.0 tCO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr (alkalinity export)
- Amann et al. (2022): Belgium mesocosm, 100 t/ha ‚Üí 3.2 tCO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr (soil inorganic carbon increase)

**Gap:** No studies have quantified ERW on **volcanic islands**, despite their ideal conditions (young basalts, high rainfall, acidic soils).

### **1.3 Why Volcanic Islands?**

Volcanic islands offer **optimal natural conditions** for ERW:

| **Factor** | **Volcanic Islands** | **Continental Agriculture** |
|------------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **Rock Availability** | Local basalt quarries | Must import rock (costly) |
| **Soil pH** | 4.5-6.0 (acidic) | 6.5-7.5 (neutral) |
| **Rainfall** | 1,500-3,000 mm/yr | 600-1,200 mm/yr |
| **Weathering Rate** | 5-15x faster | Baseline |
| **Alkalinity Export** | High drainage flux | Often low |
| **Farmer Motivation** | Need Mg, lime already | Variable |

**S√£o Miguel Island** is an ideal case study:
- **Geology:** Young (<1 Ma) basaltic volcanoes with reactive minerals (olivine, pyroxene)
- **Climate:** Subtropical oceanic (1,750 mm/yr rain, 18¬∞C mean temp), no dry season
- **Soils:** Acidic Andisols (pH 5.2-6.0), high organic matter (6-12%), Mg-deficient
- **Agriculture:** 15,000 ha intensive dairy pasture, farmers already apply 3,000 kg lime/ha/yr
- **Infrastructure:** Multiple basalt quarries, agricultural extension network
- **Baseline Data:** Real soil analysis from 11 plots (Terra Verde Association, 2024)

**Global Replicability:** If successful, this model applies to 100+ volcanic islands (Hawaii, Canaries, Cape Verde, Fiji, etc.) with potential for 1-5 MtCO‚ÇÇ/yr global removal.

### **1.4 Research Novelty**

This PhD will be the **first** to:

## **3. METHODOLOGY**

### **3.2 Experimental Design**

#### **Field Experiment Layout:**

**11 plots √ó 0.5 ha each (total: 5.5 ha)**

**Treatments (Randomized Complete Block Design):**
1. **Control** (n=3 plots): No basalt, conventional lime (3,000 kg/ha/yr) ‚Äî BASELINE
2. **Lime Replacement** (n=3): Basalt 2.7 t/ha/yr (replaces lime) ‚Äî CONSERVATIVE
3. **Moderate ERW** (n=3): Basalt 10 t/ha (one-time application) ‚Äî MODERATE
4. **Full ERW** (n=2): Basalt 50 t/ha (one-time application) ‚Äî AGGRESSIVE

**Basalt Specifications:**
- Source: Local S√£o Miguel quarries (3-5 sources to be characterized)
- Particle size: <100 ¬µm median (d50), <2 mm maximum
- Application method: Broadcast spreader (tractor-mounted), March 2027 (pre-wet season)
- Quality control: XRF analysis (major elements), ICP-MS (trace metals), BET-N‚ÇÇ (surface area)

#### **Monitoring Infrastructure:**

**Each plot equipped with:**
1. **Zero-tension lysimeters** (n=3 per plot, 60 cm depth) ‚Üí Soil solution sampling
2. **Tipping-bucket rain gauge** (n=1 per plot) ‚Üí Precipitation measurement
3. **Soil temperature/moisture probes** (n=3 per plot, 10-30-50 cm depths) ‚Üí Environmental data
4. **Groundwater monitoring well** (n=1 per treatment, 2 m depth) ‚Üí Trace metal assessment

**Total equipment:** 33 lysimeters, 11 rain gauges, 33 TDR probes, 4 monitoring wells

**Frequency:** Monthly (n=36 samples/year √ó 3 years = 108 samples/plot)

**Collection:** Zero-tension lysimeters (250 mL amber bottles, refrigerated at 4¬∞C)

**Field Measurements (within 2 hours):**

- **Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC):** Infrared CO‚ÇÇ analyzer (¬±2%)
- **Anions:** Ion chromatography (Cl‚Åª, SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª, NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª; ¬±5%)
- **Trace metals:** ICP-MS (Ni, Cr, As, Pb; ¬±10%)

**Quality Control:**
- Blanks (deionized water) every 10 samples
- Certified reference materials (CRM, e.g., NIST 1643e) every 20 samples
- Duplicate samples: 10% of total
- Charge balance error: <5% acceptance criterion

### **3.4 Geochemical Modeling**

#### **3.4.1 Speciation Modeling (PHREEQC)**

**Purpose:** Calculate equilibrium chemistry, saturation indices, DIC speciation

**Input:** Measured soil solution composition (pH, T, Ca, Mg, DIC, etc.)

**Output:**
- Saturation indices: SI(Calcite), SI(Magnesite), SI(Dolomite), SI(Quartz)
- DIC speciation: [H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ*], [HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª], [CO‚ÇÉ¬≤‚Åª]
- Alkalinity partitioning
- Free metal activities (Al¬≥‚Å∫, Fe¬≤‚Å∫/Fe¬≥‚Å∫)

**Critical Assessment:** If SI(Calcite) > 0 ‚Üí Secondary carbonate precipitation ‚Üí Reduced CO‚ÇÇ removal credit

#### **3.4.2 Reactive Transport Modeling (CrunchFlow)**

**Purpose:** Simulate time-dependent weathering, alkalinity generation, and export

**Setup:**
- 1D vertical soil column (0-60 cm, 6 cells √ó 10 cm)
- Input: Basalt dissolution kinetics (from lab experiments)
- Boundary conditions: Constant rainfall (top), free drainage (bottom)
- Run duration: 10 years (daily time steps)

**Output:**
- Alkalinity flux at bottom boundary (mol/m¬≤/yr) ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ removal
- Soil pH evolution over time
- Mg¬≤‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫ concentration profiles
- Secondary mineral precipitation (calcite, smectite clay)

**Validation:** Compare model predictions to Year 1-3 field data

#### **3.4.3 Kinetic Rate Laws**

**Forsterite (Mg‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÑ) dissolution:**
$$
r = k \cdot A \cdot [H^+]^{0.5} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{Q}{K}\right)
$$

Where:
- $k$ = rate constant (mol/m¬≤/s; from Pokrovsky & Schott, 2000)
- $A$ = reactive surface area (m¬≤/g; from BET-N‚ÇÇ)
- $[H^+]$ = proton activity
- $Q/K$ = saturation state

**Parameterization:** Use literature values, adjust for S√£o Miguel conditions (pH, T, pCO‚ÇÇ)

### **3.5 Carbon Accounting & Life Cycle Assessment**

#### **3.5.1 CO‚ÇÇ Removal Calculation**

**Primary Method:** Alkalinity Export (Direct MRV)

$$
\text{CO}_2 \text{ removal (kg/ha/yr)} = \frac{\Delta \text{Alkalinity (meq/L)} \times \text{Drainage Flux (L/ha/yr)}}{2} \times \frac{44}{1000}
$$

Where:
- ŒîAlkalinity = (Treatment - Control) alkalinity
- Drainage Flux = Precipitation - Evapotranspiration (from lysimeter volumes)
- Factor of 2: alkalinity:CO‚ÇÇ stoichiometry
- 44: molecular weight of CO‚ÇÇ

**Secondary Method:** Soil Inorganic Carbon (SIC) Accumulation

$$
\text{CO}_2 \text{ in soil (kg/ha/yr)} = \Delta \text{SIC (kg/ha/yr)} \times \frac{44}{12}
$$

(Uncertainty: SIC may reverse over time, so NOT counted as primary removal)

#### **3.5.2 Upstream Emissions (LCA)**

**System Boundaries:** Quarry ‚Üí Crushing ‚Üí Transport ‚Üí Application

| **Process** | **Emission Factor** | **Data Source** | **Uncertainty** |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Quarrying | 10 kg CO‚ÇÇ/t | Ecoinvent 3.8 | ¬±30% |
| Crushing/grinding | 40 kg CO‚ÇÇ/t | Strefler et al. 2018 | ¬±40% |
| Transport (20 km, diesel) | 5 kg CO‚ÇÇ/t | EU fuel standards | ¬±20% |
| **TOTAL** | **55 kg CO‚ÇÇ/t** | | **¬±35%** |

**Calculation:**
```
For 2.7 t/ha/yr: Upstream = 55 √ó 2.7 = 149 kg CO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr
For 50 t/ha: Upstream = 55 √ó 50 / 10 years = 275 kg CO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr (amortized)
```

#### **3.5.3 Net CO‚ÇÇ Removal**

$$
\text{Net Removal} = \text{Gross Removal (alkalinity export)} - \text{Upstream Emissions} - \text{Avoided Emissions}
$$

**Conservative Approach:** Do NOT count avoided lime emissions (farmer co-benefit, not CDR)

### **3.6 Uncertainty Quantification**

#### **3.6.1 Sources of Uncertainty**

| **Source** | **Type** | **Magnitude** | **Quantification Method** |
|------------|----------|---------------|---------------------------|
| Alkalinity measurement | Analytical | ¬±5% | Replicate titrations, CRM |
| Drainage flux estimate | Hydrologic | ¬±20% | Multiple lysimeters, water balance |
| Spatial variability | Geostatistical | ¬±30% | ANOVA, semivariograms |
| Temporal variability | Seasonal | ¬±25% | Monthly sampling, time-series analysis |
| Baseline subtraction | Control plot | ¬±10% | Paired t-tests |
| LCA emissions | Literature | ¬±35% | Monte Carlo (literature ranges) |

#### **3.6.2 Monte Carlo Simulation**

**Approach:** 10,000 random draws from probability distributions of uncertain parameters

**Software:** Python (NumPy, SciPy), R (mc2d package)

**Output:**
- Mean net CO‚ÇÇ removal (tCO‚ÇÇ/ha/yr)
- 95% confidence interval
- Probability of net positive removal: P(Net > 0)

**Target Uncertainty:** <30% (95% CI) after Year 3 (acceptable for carbon credit certification)
